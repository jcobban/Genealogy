1851 Census of Canada: Update: Help

This form is used to identify a specific page of the 1851 census of Canada to be transcribed, or to update the transcription.

In 1851 the Imperial Census of British North America covered 3 colonies: Nova Scotia, New Brunswick, and Canada. The Colony of Canada was divided administratively into Canada East, formerly known as Lower Canada and entirely contained within the modern province of Quebec, and Canada West, formerly known as Upper Canada and entirely contained within the modern province of Ontario.

In 1851 the implementation of the constitutional changes in the Colony of Canada recommended by the Durham Report was in mid-stream. This was an extremely complicated process particularly in Canada West, whose administration had been quite primitive up until now. In particular although counties and townships had been laid out on the map in most of the province they had no administrative existence. In setting up these two administrative levels it had been in many cases realized that organizations that looked fine on a map laid out in a boardroom at Westminster were unworkable in the field. So the boundaries of counties had to be redrawn, new counties created out of old, in some cases merging two paper counties into a single administrative county, as in Lennox & Addington, and in many cases even splitting individual townships up between neighbouring counties. So the Imperial Census was postponed in the Colony of Canada until 1852.

The census is divided into enumeration districts. Each district corresponds to a county. Each district was uniquely numbered in alphabetical order within province by the later Dominion Bureau of Statistics when the census was microfilmed in the 1950s and the original paper documents discarded.

Each enumeration district was subdivided into sub-districts, generally corresponding to municipalities (townships, towns, villages, wards within cities). Each sub-district was assigned a sequential number within the province, note not within the district, when the census was microfilmed in the 1950s.

If the population of a sub-district was too large to be handled by a single enumerator, the sub-district was divided into enumeration divisions, usually identified numerically, although in some cases identified by ward names even in rural townships which were not administratively divided into wards.

Within the enumeration division pages are identified numerically. This is messy because although most enumerators numbered the pages within their divisions, some did not. Furthermore since each census entry spanned both sides of a sheet of paper some enumerators incremented their page numbers for both sides of the sheet although most only at the beginning of a set of new entries. To complicate matters the enumeration sheets were unbound when delivered to the enumerator, and only bound together when they were archived after the census. In the mean time if the enumerator had not numbered them they generally got out of order, most commonly in almost exact reverse order. This creates a nightmare when a single household spans more than one page, because the continuation of the household is almost never on the numerically next page! When the census was microfilmed in the 1950s the pages were sequentially numbered within each sub-district, not division, using a rubber stamp. Again the structure of the entries caused complications. Most of the time the number increments only at the beginning of a set of new entries, but sometime it increments every time the page was turned, that is the page number increments by 2 for each set of new entries.

The application requires that you:

  1. Optionally identify the province to restrict the displayed list of districts (presented alphabetically by district name).
  2. Identify the district by selecting from the displayed list.
  3. Select the sub-district from the list displayed once you have selected a district.
  4. If the sub-district is divided into multiple divisions, a selection list is presented from which you must select a division.
  5. Select the page from the selection list of page numbers that is presented once you have selected the enumeration division. For consistency the numbering scheme from the microfilming stage, from the rubber-stamped numbers, is used.

Once you have selected the page, click on the Request Form button. The application displays a data entry form that can be updated like a spreadsheet. This form matches the layout of the original census image. If the page has already been transcribed this form contains the current contents of the transcription.

Immediately following the Request Form button there is a Progress Display button. Click on this to display an overview of the transcription status of the 1851 census. This shows the district by district progress of the transcription effort. It also provides an alternate method to identify a page to view, transcribe, or update within divisions for which the transcription has already begun.
