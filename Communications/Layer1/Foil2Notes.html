There are several characteristics which determine the suitability of a physical layer implementation to a particular application.

Delay

Delay is the time it takes for each bit of information to reach the other end of the medium. For example if you send the information by surface mail the delay is typically two to three days within North America. If you send the information by courier the delay is several hours. If you use sound to transmit the message, as has been done by the yodelers of Switzerland or the whistling shepherds of the Canary Islands, the delay is about 3 seconds per kilometer. If you use electromagnetic radiation, including signals in an electrically conductive wire, radio waves, or optical signals, then the delay is 3 to 5 microseconds per kilometer, plus delays through electronic components along the route.

The delay increases with distance. For example the delay in a copper wire stretching 5,000 km is about 25 milliseconds. The delay in a radio signal sent between two ground stations via a satellite in geo-synchronous orbit is about ¼ second.

Bandwidth

Bandwidth, expressed in Hz (sec-1), is the range of frequencies which the transmission channel can carry. This restriction may be a consequence of the physical nature of the media, or may be imposed technically to prevent the information in one channel from interfering with another channel, or may be as a result of regulations dividing up the total available bandwidth. For example a telephone circuit is designed to carry a 3kHz range of frequencies. An FM radio station, to provide higher quality stereophonic audio, has a bandwidth of 200kHz. A NTSC television channel has a bandwidth of about 3MHz to be able to represent an analog video image changing 30 times a second, as well as an analog FM audio channel. The bandwidth of a copper wire or fibre-optic cable is limited by distortion, which increases with distance, and, for copper, to prevent the generation of electromagnetic interference.

Signal/Noise Ratio

The signal to noise ratio, usually expressed in decibels, is a measure of the quality of the channel. Signal is a transmission that carries the desired information. Noise is any transmission that is either meaningless or carries undesired information. Noise is introduced in transmitters and receivers primarily as a result of the motion of molecules in the equipment due to temperature. In highly sensitive equipment, for example as used in radio astrononomy, the receivers are chilled to within a few degrees of absolute zero to minimize this. In transmission through space, including terrestrial microwave transmission, noise also comes from the environment. All information signals are carried through a noisy environment. In 1948 Claude Shannon developed the fundamental theorem of information theory that the maximum capacity of of a channel depends upon the bandwidth and the signal to noise ratio. Increasing the capacity requires either increasing the signal strength or bandwidth, which in many cases is prohibited, or reducing the strength of the noise. For example noise can be reduced in a copper wire by shielding it from external signals, for example in a coaxial cable. One of the big advantages of fiber optical transmission is that it is so easy to reduce noise.

Error Rate

Error Rate is expressed as the fraction of bits which are typically modified by the transmission channel. Shannon's Law shows that as you try to increase capacity beyond the maximum capacity permitted by the signal/noise ratio, the error rate increases so that the amount of information getting through goes down. Modern terrestrial transmission systems have typical error rates of 1 bit error in a hundred million or a billion (10-8 or 10-9).

Capacity

The capacity of a channel is the number of information bits it can carry in a second, expressed in Hz (sec-1or b/sec). For example a dial-up modem may support 56Kb/sec1, an ADSL high speed “modem” may support 1Mb/sec, and an Ethernet Local Area Network may support 100Mb/sec.

Note that occasionally the capacity may be expressed in terms of Baud. Baud, from the early French telegrapher Jean-Maurice-Emile Baudot (1845-1903), properly represents the number of signal transitions per second. Since there may either be multiple signal transitions per bit transmitted, or alternatively multiple bits carried per signal transition, this terminology is misleading and has gone out of fashion.



1Note that all telephone systems carry “voice” circuits over a digital multiplexed network. In telephone networks based upon North American standards the backbone carries a voice channel using a 56Kb/s data channel and an 8Kb/s control channel. In telephone networks based upon European standards the voice channel uses 64Kb/s and shares a 64Kb/s control channel with 29 other voice channels.
